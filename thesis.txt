Chapter 1
Introduction
Introduction
1.1 
Background 
Blockchain technology is a digital system for recording transactions in a shared ledger that is maintained by many computers rather than by one central authority. Because multiple participants store and verify the same transaction history, blockchain systems can provide transparency, data integrity, and resistance to unauthorized modification. Ethereum is one of the most widely adopted blockchain platforms. Unlike earlier blockchain systems that mainly supported simple digital currency transfers, Ethereum enables the execution of smart contracts, which are self-executing programs deployed on the blockchain. Through these smart contracts, users can exchange digital assets, access decentralized finance (DeFi) services, and perform a wide range of financial operations without depending on traditional intermediaries such as banks or brokers.
When a user initiates an Ethereum transaction, such as transferring tokens or executing a decentralized exchange trade, the transaction is broadcast to the peer-to-peer network and temporarily stored in the mempool. The mempool functions as a public queue of pending transactions that have been validated by network nodes but have not yet been included in a block. Transactions remain in this pool until they are selected during block construction. Although Ethereum validates transaction correctness, it does not guarantee a fixed or fair ordering of pending transactions. Consequently, block producers, builders, and other economically motivated participants may influence transaction placement according to incentives, network conditions, or anticipated profit opportunities.
This flexibility in transaction ordering has important implications for decentralized financial applications. In DeFi systems, the position of a transaction within a block can directly influence execution outcomes, including the exchange rate obtained, the quantity of assets received, and the overall value of a trade. These effects are particularly significant in automated market maker (AMM)-based decentralized exchanges, where asset prices are determined algorithmically according to liquidity-pool reserves. Because each trade modifies the reserve balances that determine subsequent prices, transaction ordering becomes economically important. As a result, a transaction executed immediately before or after another trade may experience a different execution price, creating opportunities for participants who can observe pending transactions and exploit predictable price movements.
The ability to extract economic value through transaction ordering manipulation is commonly referred to as Maximal Extractable Value (MEV). MEV represents the value that can be obtained by reordering, inserting, or excluding transactions during block construction [3], [6]. Within DeFi ecosystems, specialized actors continuously monitor pending transactions in search of profitable ordering opportunities. Earlier forms of MEV extraction were frequently associated with priority gas auctions, where participants competed by increasing transaction fees to secure advantageous placement. More recently, Ethereum has evolved toward structured block-building markets through infrastructures such as Flashbots and Proposer–Builder Separation (PBS), which separate block construction from block proposal and introduce competitive builder markets [32]. Although these developments reduce some inefficiencies associated with public gas auctions, research indicates that transaction information may still leak or remain exploitable under certain conditions [28].
Among the various forms of MEV observed in decentralized finance, sandwich attacks are widely regarded as one of the most harmful to ordinary users. MEV activities are commonly categorized into frontrunning, backrunning, and sandwich attacks [37]. A sandwich attack occurs when an adversary detects a pending victim transaction and strategically places two transactions around it. The attacker first submits a transaction before the victim’s trade to influence the market price and then submits a second transaction after the victim’s trade to close the position and realize a profit. Because AMM pricing mechanisms are deterministic, attackers can estimate the expected price impact of a victim transaction before it is confirmed on-chain. This predictability enables the attacker to profit from the induced price movement while causing the victim’s trade to execute at a less favorable rate [2], [4], [5]. Empirical measurement studies estimate that sandwich attacks were associated with approximately 152 million USD in user losses during 2024 [9], [30], highlighting the practical significance of the threat.
Despite increasing research attention and the development of multiple mitigation strategies, protecting users from sandwich attacks remains a challenging problem. Existing approaches provide only partial protection and often depend on private relay infrastructures, protocol-level modifications, specialized execution environments, or post-event detection mechanisms that operate after value extraction has already occurred [1], [8]. Consequently, many solutions do not address the earliest stage of the transaction lifecycle, namely the period before a transaction is publicly exposed to potential adversaries through the mempool. This limitation creates a protection gap between transaction creation and transaction 
broadcast. Addressing this gap requires a mechanism capable of evaluating transaction-level exposure before submission and providing risk-aware guidance while remaining compatible with existing Ethereum infrastructure and decentralized exchange protocols.

1.2 Research Motivation
The motivation for this research is driven by the urgent need to protect users from the escalating financial impact of Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) (Table 1.1) exploitation. While decentralized
 finance (DeFi) (Table 1.1) enables innovation, it has also created an environment where adversarial actors systematically extract value from ordinary 
participants. Sandwich attacks (Table 1.1) have evolved from theoretical anomalies into a dominant revenue source for bots, causing direct financial losses. Empirical analyses estimate these attacks were responsible for approximately $152 million in user losses during 2024 alone [9].
Users currently face a protection gap: they either rely on post-detection systems that identify theft only after it is irreversible, or depend on private relay infrastructures that remain difficult to access and still exhibit measurable vulnerabilities. Despite extensive mitigation research, real-world losses persist. This research is therefore motivated by the need to shift from reactive forensics to proactive, client-side prevention in order to better 
protect DeFi participants.
1.3 Research Importance
MEV represents a structural coordination problem in decentralized blockchain systems, where rational profit-seeking behavior by network participants can negatively affect ordinary users and undermine market fairness. In Ethereum-based decentralized finance, transaction ordering can influence execution price, received output, and user loss. This makes sandwich attacks a significant security and economic concern, particularly because adversaries can exploit transaction visibility, automated market maker pricing, and ordering opportunities to extract value from user trades [2], [5]. Although existing MEV mitigation approaches provide partial protection, many remain constrained by deployment complexity, trust assumptions, or reactive operation. Protocol-level and consensus-layer solutions often require broad ecosystem coordination and may be difficult to deploy within existing Ethereum infrastructure. Relay-based solutions, such as private transaction channels, can reduce public mempool exposure but may introduce dependence on external infrastructure and do not fully eliminate concerns related to metadata leakage or builder concentration [28]. Post-execution detection systems remain useful for measurement, auditing, and forensic analysis; however, they identify attacks only after value extraction has already occurred [8]. These limitations demonstrate the importance of protection mechanisms that operate earlier in the transaction lifecycle. A client-side pre-broadcast framework is important because it allows transaction exposure to be evaluated before the transaction becomes publicly visible in the mempool. At this stage, the user still retains control over whether to proceed, delay, or prevent transaction submission based on the assessed level of risk. Therefore, pre-broadcast evaluation provides a practical direction for reducing exposure to sandwich attack 
conditions without requiring changes to Ethereum consensus rules, deployed smart contracts, or decentralized exchange protocols.
This research addresses this gap by introducing the PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework (PADGF), a client-side pre-broadcast risk evaluation architecture designed to assess transaction exposure before network submission. PADGF evaluates transaction exposure using simulation-derived indicators that capture both victim-side output degradation and attacker-side economic viability. These indicators include victim output loss, slippage deviation, attacker gross profit, attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, profitability ratio, and a normalized transaction risk score. Gas-related conditions are treated as part of attacker cost estimation rather than as an independent risk-increasing factor. Based on the normalized risk score, the framework applies a threshold-based decision policy to classify the transaction as Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options. In this way, PADGF contributes an infrastructure-compatible mechanism for supporting risk-aware transaction decisions before broadcast. Its importance does not lie in eliminating MEV, guaranteeing transaction safety, controlling block ordering, or preventing all sandwich attacks. Instead, it provides a structured client-side mechanism for identifying economically attractive and potentially harmful transaction conditions before public exposure. This supports the broader objective of improving user protection in decentralized finance while preserving compatibility with existing Ethereum and decentralized exchange infrastructure
.
1.4 Problem Statement
Despite the deployment of Proposer-Builder Separation (PBS) and the widespread adoption of private relay infrastructures such as Flashbots, transaction ordering exploitation remains a critical issue. Empirical evidence indicates that builder concentration has increased following the adoption of these mechanisms, while sandwich attacks generated an estimated $152 million in user losses during 2024 alone, demonstrating that current defenses remain insufficient to eliminate economic extraction [9].
Existing mitigation strategies exhibit significant operational limitations. Private relay mechanisms, while designed to reduce public mempool exposure, can still introduce residual vulnerabilities related to metadata leakage, dependency on external infrastructure, or asymmetric denial-of-service conditions [27], [28]. Most existing detection mechanisms are also fundamentally reactive, operating post-confirmation and identifying sandwich attacks 
only after value extraction has occurred. These limitations show that protection mechanisms are still needed at the earliest stage of the transaction lifecycle.
These observations highlight a clear architectural gap: the absence of a lightweight, client-side pre-broadcast decision framework capable of evaluating transaction-level exposure to sandwich attack conditions before network submission. There remains a need for a mechanism that can assess transaction risk without relying entirely on trusted intermediary infrastructures or requiring modifications to Ethereum's core protocol or deployed smart contracts.
Research Question
:
 
How can a client-side pre-broadcast decision framework evaluate transaction-level exposure to sandwich attack conditions before Ethereum DeFi transactions enter the public mempool?
1.5 Research Objectives
The primary aim of this research is to design, implement, and evaluate a client-side pre-broadcast risk evaluation framework capable of reducing user exposure to sandwich attack conditions in Ethereum-based decentralized finance transactions. The framework is designed to operate within existing Ethereum infrastructure without modifying Ethereum consensus rules, deployed smart contracts, or decentralized exchange protocols. Instead, it evaluates transaction-level exposure before broadcast through simulation-derived indicators that capture both victim-side output degradation and attacker-side economic viability, and then produces a structured execution decision. To achieve this aim, the study pursues the following objectives:
To analyze
 the structural and operational limitations of existing MEV and sandwich attack mitigation approaches, including private relay mechanisms, Proposer–Builder Separation (PBS), protocol-level defenses, smart contract-level safeguards, static slippage controls, and post-execution detection systems, in order to identify the remaining gap for client-side pre-broadcast protection.
To design
 and develop the PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework (PADGF) as a client-side pre-broadcast risk evaluation framework that can receive transaction parameters, obtain a reference output, perform local transaction simulation, calculate victim-side and attacker-side indicators including victim output loss, slippage deviation, attacker gross profit, attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, and profitability ratio, and combine these 
indicators into a normalized transaction risk score before the transaction becomes visible in the public mempool.
To evaluate
 the implemented PADGF prototype under controlled forked-state experimental conditions using baseline swap execution, controlled sandwich attack simulation, and PADGF-protected evaluation, in order to assess whether the framework can generate reproducible outputs, measure adversarial output degradation, estimate attacker economic viability, and produce structured pre-broadcast risk-aware decisions.
Research Contributions
This thesis makes the following contributions to the field of blockchain security and MEV mitigation:
A Client-Side Pre-Broadcast Risk Evaluation Architecture.
This research introduces the PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework (PADGF), a client-side pre-broadcast architecture designed to evaluate transaction-level exposure before Ethereum DeFi transactions become visible in the public mempool. The framework operates externally from Ethereum consensus rules, deployed smart contracts, and decentralized exchange protocols. It is implemented as a controlled prototype using a deterministic Ethereum mainnet fork, Ethers.js, and Uniswap interaction logic, allowing transaction behavior to be assessed before broadcast without requiring protocol-level or smart-contract modification.
A 
Simulation-Based Transaction Exposure Evaluation Model.
This thesis contributes a simulation-based evaluation model that assesses transaction exposure before broadcast by estimating both victim-side output degradation and attacker-side economic viability. The model calculates measurable indicators including victim output loss, slippage deviation, attacker gross profit, attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, profitability ratio, and a normalized transaction risk score. Gas-related conditions are treated as part of attacker cost estimation rather than as an independent risk-increasing factor. This contribution provides a systematic method for estimating whether a transaction presents economically attractive and potentially harmful conditions before submission to the public mempool.
A Threshold-Based Adaptive Decision Engine.
This research introduces an adaptive decision mechanism that converts the normalized transaction risk score into one of three outcomes: Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options. The decision is derived from configured threshold values rather than manual system selection. This enables PADGF to operate as a risk-aware pre-submission decision layer, allowing low-exposure transactions to proceed, moderate-exposure transactions to be delayed for reassessment, and high-exposure transactions to be stopped from automatic public broadcast while presenting controlled user options.
Controlled Empirical Evidence of Prototype Feasibility.
This thesis provides controlled empirical evidence showing that a client-side pre-broadcast transaction evaluation workflow can be implemented and assessed under reproducible forked-state conditions. The prototype supports baseline swap execution, controlled sandwich attack simulation, and PADGF-protected evaluation. The evaluation demonstrates that the framework can generate structured outputs, measure transaction-output degradation under controlled adversarial ordering, estimate attacker economic viability, and produce a risk-aware decision before execution.
1.7 General Methodology
This research adopts a structured analytical, design-oriented, and experimental methodology to investigate client-side pre-broadcast protection against sandwich attack conditions in Ethereum-based decentralized finance. The objective is threefold: first, to examine and classify existing approaches for mitigating Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) and sandwich attacks; second, to design and implement a lightweight client-side framework capable of evaluating transaction-level exposure before public mempool submission; and third, to evaluate the implemented prototype under controlled forked-state experimental conditions. The methodology is therefore not limited to conceptual framework development. It also includes prototype implementation and controlled evaluation in order to determine whether the proposed PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework (PADGF) can generate reproducible indicators and structured pre-broadcast decisions.
The methodology is organized into three complementary phases. The first phase involves a systematic analysis of academic literature using the PRISMA framework to identify, filter, and classify relevant studies. The reviewed literature focuses primarily on MEV and transaction-ordering behavior [6], as well as sandwich attack mechanisms and their economic impact in decentralized finance [30]. This phase is further supported by 
bibliometric analysis using VOSviewer to examine thematic relationships and dominant research directions within the MEV mitigation literature. The purpose of this phase is to establish the research landscape, classify existing mitigation strategies across architectural layers, and identify the remaining gap for client-side pre-broadcast protection.
The second phase focuses on the technical analysis of existing practical mechanisms used in real-world decentralized finance environments. This phase examines mechanisms such as pre-trade simulation, slippage control, execution safeguards, liquidity-aware transaction assessment, and private transaction submission channels. The purpose is not to reproduce or directly implement these tools, but to understand how transaction exposure is currently estimated or reduced in practice. This analysis provides the technical basis for identifying the limitations of standalone solutions and informs the design requirements of PADGF as an integrated pre-broadcast decision layer.
The third phase involves the design, implementation, and controlled evaluation of PADGF. The framework is designed as a client-side architecture that operates before transaction broadcast by receiving transaction parameters, obtaining a reference output, performing local transaction simulation, calculating victim-side and attacker-side evaluation indicators, computing a normalized transaction risk score, and applying threshold-based decision control. The implemented prototype evaluates transaction exposure using indicators such as victim output loss, slippage deviation, attacker gross profit, attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, profitability ratio, and normalized risk score. Gas-related conditions are treated as part of attacker cost estimation rather than as an independent risk-increasing factor. The prototype is evaluated using baseline swap execution, controlled sandwich attack simulation, and PADGF-protected evaluation under reproducible forked-state conditions. This methodological structure ensures that the proposed framework is assessed as a client-side pre-broadcast decision-support mechanism rather than as a protocol modification, decentralized exchange redesign, or post-execution detection tool.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
Literature Review
2.1 Introduction
This chapter reviews existing research on Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) and mitigation mechanisms within decentralized finance (DeFi). Although Ethereum ensures transaction validity and state consistency, it does not guarantee fair transaction ordering, allowing economically motivated reordering strategies that extract value from users. MEV development can be broadly characterized in three phases: an early phase dominated by Priority Gas Auctions (PGAs), where bots competed through escalating transaction fees to obtain favorable ordering; a second phase marked by the introduction of Proposer-Builder Separation (PBS) and private relay infrastructures such as Flashbots, which formalized MEV markets and separated block construction from block proposal; and a recent phase extending MEV dynamics into Layer-2 rollups and cross-domain environments, increasing transaction-ordering complexity and expanding extraction opportunities [38].
Among MEV strategies, sandwich attacks remain one of the most economically significant forms, with approximately $152 million in recorded user losses during 2024 [9]. Despite the introduction of private relay infrastructures and detection systems, empirical studies indicate that users remain exposed to transaction-ordering risks and metadata leakage under certain network conditions [27], [28]. Existing mitigation approaches primarily operate at the protocol layer through mechanisms such as encrypted mempools, at the smart contract or application layer through techniques such as slippage optimization, or at the post-detection stage using analytical and classification frameworks [1]. Comparatively little attention has been given to client-side mitigation mechanisms that operate prior to transaction broadcast without requiring consensus modification. Accordingly, this chapter reviews the current literature, categorizes existing mitigation approaches, and identifies the architectural gap that motivates the proposed PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework (PADGF).
2.2 Search Methodology for Literature Review
To ensure a systematic and reproducible review process, this study adopts a two-stage methodology combining structured study selection and bibliometric analysis. First, relevant studies are identified and filtered using the PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) framework. Second, the selected literature is analyzed using VOSviewer to examine thematic relationships and research trends within the field of MEV mitigation.
2.2.1 Study Selection Using PRISMA
The selection of relevant studies followed the PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) framework, which provides a structured and widely accepted methodology for conducting systematic literature reviews. PRISMA organizes the study selection process into three sequential stages: identification, screening, and eligibility assessment. This structured approach helps ensure that the literature review is conducted in a transparent, systematic, and reproducible manner. By documenting the criteria used at each stage of the filtering process, the framework allows researchers to clearly justify the inclusion and exclusion of studies while minimizing potential selection bias in the final dataset of reviewed publications.
Stage 1: Identification of Relevant Literature
The first stage aimed to identify academic studies related to Maximal Extractable Value (MEV), sandwich attacks, and transaction-ordering manipulation in blockchain systems. Searches were performed across major academic databases and conference repositories, including IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, SpringerLink, ScienceDirect (Elsevier), and the proceedings of the USENIX Security Symposium. Google Scholar was additionally used for citation tracking and verification of frequently cited publications.
The search was limited to publications from 2020 to 2025 to capture research emerging after the rapid growth of decentralized finance and the introduction of infrastructure such as Flashbots and Proposer-Builder Separation. Boolean combinations of domain-specific keywords were used to construct search queries, including "Maximal Extractable Value" or "MEV," "sandwich attack," "frontrunning," and "transaction reordering." These keywords were combined with related terms such as "Flashbots," "private transaction pools," "Proposer-Builder Separation," "threshold encryption," "mempool security," "cross-domain MEV," and "slippage control." The initial database search identified 115 records. After removing 15 records before screening because they were non-peer-reviewed sources or non-English papers, 100 records were screened.
 
During this stage, each record was catalogued with basic bibliographic information, keywords, and a brief summary of its contribution. This documentation facilitated consistent evaluation during the subsequent filtering stages.
Stage 2: Screening and Relevance Filtering
During the screening stage, the titles and abstracts of the identified records were examined to assess their relevance to the research scope. The purpose of this step was to eliminate 
studies that did not directly address MEV extraction, transaction-ordering behavior, or mitigation mechanisms within Ethereum or EVM-compatible blockchain environments. Studies were retained if they provided technical analysis, empirical measurement, formal modeling, or architectural proposals related to MEV. Publications consisting primarily of general commentary, market-oriented discussions, or industry reports lacking technical depth were excluded, as were works addressing unrelated blockchain topics. After applying these screening criteria, 36 studies remained and were selected for full-text evaluation.
Stage 3: Eligibility Assessment and Final Selection
The remaining studies were subjected to full-text review to evaluate their methodological quality and relevance to the research objectives. Each paper was analyzed with respect to its technical approach, threat model, and the architectural layer at which the proposed mechanism operates. Particular attention was given to whether mitigation strategies were implemented at the client, mempool, relay, smart contract, or consensus layer, as well as to factors such as latency constraints, cryptographic requirements, dependence on protocol modifications, and compatibility with existing Ethereum infrastructure. Following this assessment, 25 studies satisfied the eligibility criteria and were retained for detailed analytical review, providing the broader context for understanding existing approaches to MEV measurement, detection, and mitigation. From this set, eight studies were identified as the most directly relevant to the research problem based on their focus on mitigation mechanisms and architectural feasibility, and these works constitute the comparative analysis presented in Table 2.1.

2.3 Evolution of Maximal Extractable Value and Taxonomy of Mitigation Strategies
Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) has undergone a clear structural evolution driven by changes in transaction ordering authority and market coordination mechanisms. In the early phase (2019–2021), MEV extraction was primarily governed by gas price auctions, where 
miners controlled
 transaction ordering and searchers competed by increasing transaction fees to gain priority. This model resulted in significant network congestion and 
inefficient fee escalation. The introduction of relay-based infrastructures, particularly Flashbots and Proposer–Builder Separation (PBS) (2021–2023), restructured this process by separating block construction from block proposal, enabling specialized builders to create optimized blocks and reducing visible mempool competition. More recent developments extend MEV beyond single-chain environments into cross-domain and Layer-2 settings, where sequencers and delayed finality introduce new dimensions of transaction ordering and coordination complexity.
While this evolution reflects increasing system sophistication, it also highlights persistent inefficiencies arising from strategic transaction manipulation. From a behavioral perspective, MEV can be interpreted as a coordination failure, where individually rational 
actions such as frontrunning or arbitrage 
lead
 to collectively suboptimal outcomes. These behaviors are typically categorized into frontrunning, backrunning, and sandwich attacks, with sandwich attacks representing one of the most economically impactful forms of MEV in decentralized finance. The continued prevalence of such strategies has motivated the development of mitigation mechanisms across multiple layers of the Ethereum architecture.

 presents a structured taxonomy of MEV mitigation strategies, organized into four architectural layers: Consensus and Mempool, Application and Smart Contract, Relay and Infrastructure, and Post-Execution Detection. At the consensus and mempool level, mitigation focuses on restricting information leakage and controlling transaction visibility through techniques such as threshold encryption (e.g., BEAT-MEV), accountable mempools (LØ), and batched transaction concealment. The application and smart contract layer addresses MEV at the level of decentralized applications by incorporating mechanisms such as anti-sandwich protections, slippage control, and execution delays to reduce exploitability within automated market makers. At the relay and infrastructure layer, systems such as Flashbots, private relays, and order flow auctions restructure transaction propagation and block construction to limit public exposure and coordinate execution more efficiently. Finally, post-execution detection mechanisms rely on pattern recognition, anomaly detection, and forensic analysis to identify MEV exploitation after transactions have been processed.

However, each layer introduces trade-offs. Protocol-level approaches may increase complexity and coordination overhead, infrastructure-based solutions can introduce centralization risks, application-level defenses are often limited in scope, and detection-based approaches remain reactive rather than preventive. The taxonomy therefore illustrates both the diversity of existing solutions and the absence of a unified approach capable of addressing MEV comprehensively across all layers. This observation reinforces the need for mechanisms that operate earlier in the transaction lifecycle while maintaining compatibility with existing Ethereum

2.5 Structural Model of MEV Exploitability 
To extend the analysis beyond descriptive mechanics, it is necessary to formalize the structural conditions

2.5.1 Transaction Ordering Structure
Let T_V represent the victim transaction submitted to the public mempool. After observing T_V, the attacker constructs two additional transactions: a front-run transaction T_A1 and a back-run transaction T_A2. Let O(T) denote the ordering position assigned to a transaction by the block builder or block construction process. A sandwich attack becomes possible when the attacker can achieve the following ordering relation:
O(T_A1) < O(T_V) < O(T_A2)
This ordering relation means that the attacker's front-run transaction is executed before the victim transaction, while the attacker's back-run transaction is executed after the victim transaction. Under AMM pricing rules, T_A1 modifies the pool reserves before T_V is processed. Consequently, T_V executes at a worse price than it would have received under the original pool state. After the victim transaction has moved the pool further, T_A2 allows the attacker to close the temporary position and extract profit from the induced price movement.
This ordering condition is essential. Without the ability to place T_A1 before T_V and T_A2 after T_V, the sandwich structure cannot be completed. Therefore, the attack depends not only on the existence of a large or price-impacting victim transaction, but also 
on the attacker's ability to exploit transaction visibility and ordering discretion.
2.5.2 Profitability Constraint
A sandwich attack is economically rational only when the expected gain from the victim-induced price movement is greater than the attacker's cost and residual risk. Let ΔP denote the price
-impact-based gain opportunity created by the victim transaction, G denote the total gas expenditure required for the attacker's front-run and back-run transactions, and R denote residual risk, such as failed inclusion, adverse ordering, or latency uncertainty. The attack is profitable when the following condition is satisfied:
ΔP > G + R
This expression shows that attackers do not target every transaction. They are more likely to target transactions where the expected price movement is large enough to cover both transaction fees and execution uncertainty. Because AMM pricing is deterministic and pending transactions reveal trade parameters before confirmation, attackers can often estimate the potential price impact and expected profitability before submitting their own transactions.
This profitability constraint also explains why price impact and slippage exposure are central to MEV analysis. A transaction that creates little price movement may not justify the attacker's gas expenditure. By contrast, a large transaction in a pool with limited liquidity may generate enough price movement to make a sandwich sequence 
economically attractive.
2.5.3 Information Asymmetry and Latency Differential
The structural vulnerability behind sandwich attacks is strengthened by information asymmetry during the pre-confirmation phase. Once a transaction is propagated to the public mempool, it becomes visible before final ordering is determined. This allows
 adversarial bots to observe the transaction, estimate its expected effect on pool reserves, and react before the transaction is included in a block.
Let L_a represent the attacker's effective observation and reaction latency, and let L_v represent the effective latency of the victim transaction until final inclusion. Exploitability increases when the attacker can react faster than the victim transaction is finalized:
L_a < L_v
When this condition holds, the attacker has enough time to construct and submit T_A1 and T_A2 with sufficient priority to influence transaction ordering. This latency advantage does not necessarily require the attacker to compromise the victim's wallet or break cryptographic assumptions. Instead, the attacker exploits the timing gap between transaction broadcast and block inclusion.
This model clarifies why pre-broadcast protection is important. Once the transaction enters the public mempool, it may become observable to latency-optimized adversarial systems. Therefore, PADGF focuses on evaluating transaction exposure before broadcast. The framework does not claim to detect a completed sandwich attack before it happens. Rather, 
it assesses whether the transaction has characteristics that may make it exploitable if it becomes visible to adversaries.
2.5.4 Structural Conditions for Exploitability
From the above formulation, sandwich attacks depend on the simultaneous presence of several structural conditions within the transaction execution environment. Specifically, these
 attacks become feasible when pending transactions are publicly visible before confirmation, allowing attackers to observe and react to them in real time. They also rely on the deterministic or reliably estimable price impact mechanisms inherent in Automated Market Makers (AMMs), which enable attackers to predict how trades will influence market 
prices. In addition, the presence of discretionary transaction ordering authority at the block builder or proposer level allows malicious actors to strategically position their transactions relative to the victim's trade. Adequate liquidity depth is also required to execute the necessary offsetting trades without causing prohibitive market disruption. Finally, attackers must possess a latency advantage that enables them to insert their transactions into the execution order before block finalization, ensuring successful front-run and back-run positioning around the victim's transaction.
Existing mitigation approaches typically weaken one or more of these properties. For instance, cryptographic solutions aim to eliminate transaction visibility, economic mechanisms reduce profitability margins, and contract-level techniques constrain execution behavior. In addition, practical tools such as pre-trade simulation and slippage control partially address exploitability by estimating transaction outcomes prior to execution. However, these mechanisms operate independently and do not form a unified protection strategy at the client stage.
 
This structural model frames sandwich attacks as an emergent consequence of transaction visibility combined with discretionary ordering authority. As a result, effective mitigation must either eliminate information leakage before broadcast or reduce exploitability through
 early-stage risk evaluation and decision control. The following section evaluates existing 
approaches in relation to these structural conditions 
2.6 Related Work
Research on Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) mitigation has evolved from empirical measurement studies toward the development of protocol-level, infrastructure-based, and application-specific defense
 mechanisms. As structured in Figure 2.4, existing approaches can be systematically classified into four architectural layers: Consensus & Mempool, Application & Smart Contract, Relay & Infrastructure, and Post-Execution
 
Detection. This layered classification reflects the different points at which mitigation mechanisms intervene within the transaction lifecycle.
Measurement studies [9][30] quantify the economic impact of sandwich attacks and establish the need for effective mitigation. However, the literature reveals a fundamental trade-off: approaches implemented at lower layers of the system (e.g., consensus or mempool) provide stronger guarantees by controlling transaction visibility and ordering, while higher-layer solutions (e.g., application-level defenses) offer better deployability but limited protection 
scope. Table 2.6.1 provides a comparative analysis of representative

2.6.1 Consensus and Mempool Level Approaches
Approaches at the consensus and mempool level aim to mitigate MEV by restricting access to transaction information
 or enforcing fair ordering mechanisms. Techniques such as threshold encryption (e.g., BEAT-MEV [35]) conceal transaction contents until ordering decisions are finalized, thereby preventing adversaries from exploiting mempool visibility. Similarly, accountable mempool designs such as LØ [14] introduce verifiable mechanisms to enforce consistent transaction ordering and detect deviations.
Critical Analysis:
These approaches address the root cause of MEV
 
transaction visibility and ordering control and therefore provide strong theoretical guarantees. However, they require substantial coordination across network participants, often involving protocol modifications or cryptographic infrastructure. This results in high deployment complexity and limits their 
feasibility within existing Ethereum systems.
2.6.2 Application and Smart Contract Level Approaches
Application-level defenses
 focus on mitigating MEV within decentralized applications, particularly automated market makers (AMMs). Techniques include anti-sandwich mechanisms embedded in smart contracts [10], slippage control strategies, and transaction optimization methods such as those proposed by Alpos et al. [11]. These approaches aim to reduce exploitability by modifying trade execution logic or constraining price impact.
Critical Analysis:
These methods are lightweight and compatible with existing infrastructure, making them easier to deploy. However, their effectiveness is limited to specific applications and does not generalize across the ecosystem. In addition, they often introduce composability challenges, as modifying smart contracts may require redeployment or changes to established DeFi protocols. Their localized nature prevents them from addressing systemic 
MEV risks.
2.6.3 Relay and Infrastructure Approaches
The emergence of Proposer–Builder Separation (PBS) has enabled relay-based mitigation strategies, with systems
 such as Flashbots [19] allowing transactions to bypass the public mempool. By submitting transactions through private relays, these approaches reduce 
exposure to adversarial monitoring and limit opportunities for frontrunning and sandwich attacks.
Critical Analysis:
Relay-based solutions offer practical deployability and require minimal changes to existing clients. However, they introduce new trust assumptions and centralization risks, as transaction flow becomes dependent on a limited number of relay operators and block 
builders. Furthermore, recent studies [27][28] indicate that metadata leakage and partial transparency can still enable adversarial inference, reducing their effectiveness.
2.6.4 Post-Execution Detection Approaches
Post-execution approaches focus on identifying MEV-related attacks after transactions have been included in the
 blockchain. Techniques include rule-based detection using client-level monitoring tools [1][8] and machine learning-based methods such as graph neural networks for identifying suspicious transaction patterns [21].
Critical Analysis:
These approaches are effective for measurement, auditing, and forensic analysis, providing valuable insights into MEV activity. However, their reactive nature represents a fundamental limitation, as they do not prevent attacks but only detect them after value extraction has occurred. Consequently, they are insufficient as standalone mitigation strategies.

2.6.5 Synthesis of Literature and Identified Architectural Gap
Based on the comparative analysis and bibliometric findings, a clear architectural gap can be identified at the pre-broadcast stage of the transaction lifecycle. While several mitigation mechanisms exist across different layers, including protocol-level solutions, relay-based approaches, and application-level safeguards, these methods are largely fragmented and address isolated aspects of the problem.
 
In particular, practical tools such as pre-trade simulation and slippage control mechanisms demonstrate the feasibility of estimating transaction risk prior to execution. However, these approaches operate as standalone components and do not provide a unified decision-making process that systematically evaluates risk and enforces protective actions before transaction submission.
To address this limitation, this research proposes the PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework (PADGF), a client-side architecture designed to integrate these mechanisms into a cohesive pre-broadcast protection layer. The framework introduces a structured decision process that combines transaction simulation, risk evaluation, and execution control to reduce exposure to sandwich attacks. By operating prior to transaction propagation, PADGF enables proactive mitigation while maintaining compatibility with existing Ethereum infrastructure and avoiding the need for protocol-level modifications.

3.1 Introduction
This chapter presents the design, implementation, and evaluation methodology of the PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework (PADGF), a client-side pre-broadcast framework proposed to reduce user exposure to sandwich attack conditions in Ethereum-based decentralized finance transactions. The chapter translates the architectural gap identified in the previous chapters into a concrete technical framework that can evaluate transaction risk before the transaction becomes visible to the public mempool. The central purpose of Chapter 3 is to explain how PADGF is structured, how its internal components interact, and how the proposed protection logic is implemented as a reproducible prototype. The framework is not designed as an Ethereum protocol modification, a decentralized exchange redesign, or a production wallet. Instead, it is developed as an external client-side decision layer that receives transaction parameters, performs pre-broadcast simulation, calculates measurable risk indicators, and determines whether the transaction should be executed normally, delayed for reassessment, or stopped from automatic public broadcast with user-controlled high-risk options.

3.2 Methodology
The methodology adopted in this chapter follows an applied cybersecurity design approach. This approach is appropriate because sandwich attacks are adversarial, transaction-ordering-dependent, and strongly influenced by the state of automated market maker liquidity pools. A purely descriptive analysis would not be sufficient to demonstrate how a mitigation mechanism can operate before transaction exposure. Therefore, PADGF is designed as a functional pre-broadcast decision framework that can receive transaction parameters, simulate expected execution behavior, evaluate risk indicators, and apply a risk-aware decision policy.
The methodology is driven by the architectural gap identified in Chapter 2. Existing mitigation approaches commonly operate at the protocol, relay, smart-contract, or post-execution detection layers. Although such approaches provide useful protections, they often require protocol changes, depend on private infrastructure, or analyze only after value extraction has already occurred. PADGF addresses this gap by placing the protection mechanism at the client layer, where the transaction can still be assessed before it enters the public mempool.
A central methodological decision is the placement of PADGF at the pre-broadcast stage of the transaction lifecycle. At this stage, the user has prepared a transaction, but the transaction has not yet been submitted to the Ethereum network. This position allows the framework to inspect transaction characteristics before adversarial actors can observe the transaction in the mempool and construct front-running or back-running strategies around it. Accordingly, the methodology treats sandwich attack mitigation from an early-stage decision-control perspective rather than from a post-execution detection perspective.

3.3 Phase 1: Framework Design
Phase 1 establishes the conceptual and architectural foundation of PADGF. The purpose of this phase is to define the protection boundary of the framework, identify the assumptions under which it operates, and specify the architectural components required for client-side 
pre-broadcast risk evaluation. This phase is necessary because PADGF is not intended to replace Ethereum infrastructure or modify decentralized exchange contracts. Instead, it is positioned as a client-side decision layer that evaluates transaction exposure before network submission.

3.3.1 Research Scope and Client-Side Objective
The scope of this research is limited to client-side pre-broadcast risk evaluation for Ethereum-based decentralized finance transactions. The framework focuses on token-swap transactions executed through automated market maker mechanisms, where transaction ordering, pool state, slippage tolerance, and gas conditions can influence the final output received by the user. The central problem addressed by PADGF is not the general elimination of MEV, but the reduction of user exposure to sandwich attack conditions before a transaction becomes publicly visible.
This scope is deliberately defined at the client layer. Once a transaction is broadcast to the public mempool, adversarial searchers may observe its parameters, estimate its price impact, and attempt to construct a sandwich sequence around it. By contrast, before broadcast, the user still has control over whether the transaction should proceed normally, be delayed and reassessed, or be stopped from automatic public broadcast when high-risk conditions are detected. PADGF therefore targets the earliest controllable point in the transaction lifecycle: the stage after transaction preparation but before network submission.
The client-side objective of PADGF is to provide a structured decision-support mechanism that evaluates transaction-level exposure before mempool visibility. To achieve this, the framework receives transaction parameters, obtains or simulates expected output, estimates relevant risk indicators, computes a normalized transaction risk score, and applies a threshold-based decision policy. The resulting decision is expressed as Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options. This decision is intended to support safer transaction submission behavior while preserving user control, because high-risk transactions are not permanently prevented; instead, automatic public broadcast is stopped and the user is presented with controlled options.
The framework is not designed to control block ordering, guarantee transaction safety, or prevent all MEV strategies. It also does not assume the ability to alter validator behavior, enforce fair ordering, or predict all builder decisions. These exclusions keep the framework focused on transaction-ordering exposure rather than unrelated security threats. The research scope also excludes wallet compromise, private-key theft, smart-contract exploitation, oracle manipulation, and consensus-layer attacks. These are important security problems, but they are outside the transaction-ordering focus of this study. PADGF specifically addresses conditions associated with sandwich attack exposure, including victim output loss, slippage deviation, attacker economic viability, and pre-broadcast decision control.
3.3.2 System Model and Threat Assumptions
The system model positions PADGF between the user and the Ethereum network. The user prepares a transaction through a client-side interface or transaction-handling process. Before the transaction is signed and broadcast, PADGF intercepts the transaction parameters and evaluates them internally. Only after the framework completes simulation, risk evaluation, and decision control may the transaction be forwarded to execution. This model separates internal transaction assessment from external network exposure.
The main entities in the model are the user, the PADGF client-side layer, the simulated Ethereum execution environment, the decentralized exchange or automated market maker, the public mempool, and the adversarial searcher. The user provides the intended swap parameters, such as token pair, input amount, slippage tolerance, and route. The PADGF layer performs the pre-broadcast evaluation. The simulated execution environment estimates transaction behavior under a state-consistent fork or RPC-supported state. The decentralized exchange supplies the AMM pricing context, while the public mempool represents the exposure point that PADGF attempts to avoid until a decision has been made.
The threat model assumes an adversary consistent with common MEV searcher behavior. The adversary can monitor public mempool transactions after they are broadcast, estimate price impact from visible transaction parameters, submit front-running and back-running transactions with sufficient priority, and attempt to influence transaction ordering. This model does not require the adversary to compromise the user wallet, steal private keys, alter the decentralized exchange contract, corrupt Ethereum consensus, or directly control all block producers. Instead, the threat model focuses on adversarial exploitation of public transaction visibility and ordering opportunities.
The model further assumes that local simulation can provide useful risk indicators before broadcast, but not absolute guarantees about final execution. Network conditions may change after simulation, and live block construction may differ from simulated conditions. Therefore, PADGF treats simulation outputs as indicators of exposure, not as deterministic proof that an attack will or will not occur. This assumption supports a risk-based decision model rather than a binary attack-detection model.
3.3.3 Design Principles
The design of PADGF is guided by principles derived from the research scope and threat model. These principles ensure that the framework remains compatible with existing Ethereum infrastructure while still providing a practical pre-broadcast risk evaluation mechanism.
The first principle is client-side deployment. PADGF is placed outside the Ethereum consensus layer, outside deployed smart contracts, and outside decentralized exchange protocol logic. This placement allows the framework to operate without requiring network-wide coordination, protocol upgrades, or contract redeployment. The protection function is performed before broadcast, where the user still retains control over transaction submission.
The second principle is pre-broadcast intervention. The framework evaluates transactions before they are submitted to the Ethereum network. This distinguishes PADGF from post-execution detection systems, which identify attacks after value extraction has already occurred. By intervening before mempool exposure, PADGF moves the protection point to the earliest stage at which the user can still delay, modify, or cancel the transaction.
The third principle is deterministic transaction simulation. Before a transaction is broadcast, the framework simulates the intended execution against a state-consistent Ethereum environment. This simulation estimates expected output, gas usage, price impact, and possible execution deviation without exposing the transaction to external observers. Because sandwich attack exposure depends heavily on AMM reserves and price impact, simulation provides the empirical basis for risk evaluation.
The fourth principle is risk-based decision control. PADGF does not assume that every transaction is either safe or actively under attack. Instead, it calculates measurable indicators and combines them into a normalized risk score. This allows transactions to be classified into different regions: low-risk transactions may proceed normally, moderate-risk 
transactions may be delayed for reassessment, and high-risk transactions stop automatic public broadcast and present controlled user options.
The fifth principle is minimal trust dependency. The framework is designed to reduce reliance on trusted intermediaries such as private relays, centralized order-flow services, or external transaction protection providers. Although private transaction channels may reduce public mempool exposure, they introduce assumptions related to relay honesty, metadata leakage, and builder concentration. PADGF keeps the primary risk evaluation and decision logic at the client side.
The sixth principle is bounded latency and usability. Since PADGF operates before transaction submission, its evaluation process must remain efficient enough to support practical decentralized finance interaction. Excessive delay could lead to stale quotes, changed liquidity conditions, or poor user experience. Therefore, the framework is designed as a lightweight pipeline in which simulation, scoring, and decision control occur within a bounded pre-broadcast interval.
The seventh principle is modularity. The framework is organized as a sequence of independent but connected components: transaction input handling, simulation, risk evaluation, decision control, and execution. This modular design improves clarity, simplifies testing, and allows individual components to be modified or extended without redesigning the entire framework. Together, these principles define PADGF as a proactive, client-side, and infrastructure-compatible mitigation framework.
3.3.4 PADGF Architecture
The PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework is designed as a modular client-side architecture for evaluating transaction exposure before broadcast. The framework follows a sequential pipeline in which transaction parameters are processed internally before any network submission occurs. This architectural separation between internal risk evaluation and external transaction broadcast is central to the security objective of PADGF.
As illustrated in Figure 3.2, the architecture begins with the Transaction Input Module. This module captures user-defined transaction parameters such as token pair, input amount, slippage tolerance, and intended execution route. Instead of immediately signing and broadcasting the transaction, the module prepares the transaction for internal simulation processing. This prevents premature exposure of transaction details to the public mempool
Figure 3.2: PADGF System Architecture
The Pre-Broadcast Simulation Module performs a dry-run execution of the intended transaction using a deterministic Ethereum execution environment. In the prototype, this environment may be represented by a forked mainnet state that preserves relevant liquidity reserves and AMM pricing behavior. The purpose of the simulation is to estimate expected output, gas usage, price movement, and execution behavior without revealing the transaction to external observers.
The Risk Evaluation Model processes the simulation output and converts it into measurable exposure and attacker-economics indicators. These indicators include victim output loss, slippage deviation, attacker gross profit, attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, profitability ratio, and the normalized risk score. Gas conditions are used to estimate attacker execution cost rather than being treated as a direct risk-increasing factor. The resulting score does not guarantee that a sandwich attack will occur; rather, it estimates whether the transaction presents economically attractive and harmful conditions if broadcast. 
The Adaptive Decision Engine interprets the normalized risk score according to the configured threshold policy. If the score is low, the engine authorizes normal execution. If the score falls into the intermediate region, the engine recommends delaying the transaction and reassessing conditions before submission. If the score exceeds the high-risk threshold, the engine stops automatic public broadcast and presents the user with controlled high-risk options: cancel the transaction, submit it through a protected private route, or proceed 
manually after receiving a clear warning. This decision mechanism ensures that PADGF does not only observe risk, but also applies a protective pre-broadcast control while preserving final user choice.
The Execution Layer is activated when the Decision Engine authorizes normal execution or when the user selects an allowed high-risk handling option. In the controlled prototype, execution occurs within the forked environment to preserve repeatability and avoid financial risk. For the high-risk private-route option, the prototype models protected submission as a local private-route execution path rather than a live relay inclusion test. In a practical deployment, the same architectural principle would apply: signing and public broadcasting should occur only after pre-broadcast approval or after the user selects a controlled alternative. In this way, the architecture preserves the core objective of PADGF, which is to keep transaction evaluation inside the client-side boundary until a risk-aware decision has been made.

3.4.2 Pre-Broadcast Transaction Simulation
The Pre-Broadcast Transaction Simulation Module evaluates the intended transaction before it is submitted to the network. This module is central to the prototype because PADGF depends on simulation-derived evidence rather than post-execution observation. The transaction is assessed while it remains under user-side control, allowing a decision to be generated before exposure to the public mempool. The simulation process first obtains a reference output for the intended swap. This value represents the expected transaction outcome under the current forked-state condition. The prototype then simulates the intended transaction locally and compares the simulated output with the reference output. This comparison provides the basis for identifying output deviation and later calculating slippage-related exposure. The simulation is conducted in a deterministic Ethereum mainnet fork environment. This environment preserves relevant on-chain contract behavior, token logic, liquidity state, and automated market maker pricing behavior while avoiding live financial risk. It also supports reproducibility, because the same forked state can be reused when comparing baseline execution, controlled attack behavior, and protected execution. The simulation module is not presented as a full reproduction of live Ethereum network behavior. It does not reproduce live public mempool competition, validator ordering decisions, private relay behavior, builder strategies, or real-time adversarial searcher activity. Its purpose is to 
provide a controlled and state-consistent execution model that supports pre-broadcast risk estimation.
3.4.3 Risk Evaluation Model
The Risk Evaluation Model processes the outputs generated by the pre-broadcast simulation module and converts them into a normalized transaction-risk score. In the revised PADGF prototype, risk evaluation is not based on gas pressure alone or on manually assigned scenario labels. Instead, the model estimates whether the transaction creates a realistic economic incentive for a potential sandwich attacker. This makes the risk score more closely aligned with the economic logic of sandwich attacks, where an attacker is expected to act only if the potential extraction value is greater than the execution cost. The model begins by comparing the reference output with the simulated output. The reference output represents the expected transaction output under the current controlled state, while the simulated output represents the transaction output after the framework evaluates possible adverse execution conditions before broadcast. The difference between these two values is recorded as the victim output loss.
Victim Output Loss = Reference Output − Simulated Output
The victim output loss measures the amount of value that the transaction initiator may lose under the evaluated conditions. This value is important because it provides the foundation for estimating whether a sandwich attack could be economically attractive. If the simulated output is equal to the reference output, the victim output loss is zero, and the transaction does not present meaningful extractable value under the evaluated condition. After calculating victim output loss, the model estimates attacker-side economic incentive using a lightweight heuristic profitability model rather than a fixed extraction-efficiency constant. This estimate considers pool fee impact, builder-bribe deduction, and attacker gas cost.
The model treats pool fees and builder bribes as reductions to potential attacker profit. Pool fees reflect swap costs paid during front-running and back-running transactions, while the builder-bribe deduction represents the portion of value that a searcher may need to pay to obtain favorable ordering. The remaining amount is reduced by estimated gas cost to produce attacker net profit. Gas therefore remains an attacker cost rather than an independent risk-increasing factor.
Attacker Net Profit = Estimated Attacker Profit − Builder Bribe − Attacker Gas Cost
The attacker net profit is the central economic indicator in the revised model. If the attacker net profit is zero or negative, the profitability component is treated as zero because the evaluated transaction is economically unattractive to a rational attacker under the configured prototype assumptions. If the attacker net profit is positive, the transaction may present an economic incentive for attack, depending on the size of the profit relative to the transaction value and the associated transaction degradation. The model also calculates slippage deviation. Slippage deviation measures the relative difference between the reference output and the simulated output. While attacker net profit represents the attacker-side incentive, slippage deviation represents the victim-side impact. Together, these two components allow PADGF to evaluate both whether the attacker has a reason to act and whether the transaction outcome is meaningfully degraded. The normalized risk score is calculated using two weighted components: the Profitability Component and the Slippage Component. The Profitability Component reflects attacker economic incentive, while the Slippage Component reflects transaction-output deterioration. In the implemented prototype, the risk score is calculated as follows:
Risk Score = (0.6 × Profitability Component) + (0.4 × Slippage Component)

3.4.3.1 Risk Indicator and Risk Score Calculation
To make the PADGF risk evaluation process reproducible, the prototype defines each risk indicator through an explicit calculation sequence. The purpose of these calculations is not to predict final Ethereum block ordering with certainty, but to estimate whether the prepared transaction presents harmful and economically attractive conditions under the configured simulation assumptions.
Let Oref represent the reference output expected under normal execution conditions, and let Osim represent the simulated output under the evaluated condition. The victim output loss is calculated as the difference between these two values. If the simulated output is equal to or greater than the reference output, the victim output loss is treated as zero because no output degradation is observed.
Victim Output Loss = 
max(
0, Oref - Osim)
Slippage deviation expresses this loss as a percentage of the reference output. It captures the victim-side impact of the evaluated transaction condition.
Slippage Deviation = (Victim Output Loss / Oref) × 100
The revised prototype estimates attacker profitability through a lightweight heuristic economic model rather than through a fixed extraction-efficiency value. The model considers pool fee impact, builder-bribe deduction, and attacker gas cost. Pool fee impact reflects the fees that an attacker would normally pay during both the front-running and back-running transactions. The builder-bribe deduction represents the portion of potential profit that may be paid to obtain favorable transaction ordering. The attacker gas cost represents the estimated cost of executing the adversarial sequence.
Attacker Net Profit = Estimated Attacker Profit − Builder Bribe − Attacker Gas Cost
Attacker net profit is the main attacker-side economic indicator. If attacker net profit is zero or negative, the profitability component is treated as zero because the transaction is not economically attractive under the configured prototype assumptions. However, victim-side output degradation remains represented through the slippage component.
Profitability Ratio = (Attacker Net Profit / Oref) × 100
The prototype then normalizes the profitability and slippage components into bounded values between 0 and 1. The profitability component represents attacker economic incentive, while the slippage component represents victim-side output deterioration.
Profitability Component = 
min(
1, max(0, Profitability Ratio / 1.0))
Slippage Component = 
min(
1, Slippage Deviation / Slippage Normalization Cap)
The value 1.0 is used as the profitability normalization cap in the implemented prototype, while the slippage normalization cap is kept constant across the reported scenarios for comparability.
Risk Score R = (0.6 × Profitability Component) + (0.4 × Slippage Component)
This means that attacker profitability receives the higher weight because sandwich attacks are primarily economically motivated, while slippage remains included because victim-side output degradation is still relevant. The revised profitability model remains a heuristic approximation rather than an exact AMM reserve-based profitability engine. It improves attacker-profitability estimation while preserving the original PADGF methodology and decision logic.
3.4.4 Adaptive Decision Engine
The Adaptive Decision Engine is the final operational component of the prototype implementation. Its role is to convert the normalized risk score into a clear pre-broadcast decision. The decision is not selected manually by the system operator; it is produced by comparing the computed risk score with configured threshold values. The implemented decision policy produces three main risk outcomes. A 
low risk
 score indicates that the transaction may proceed normally. A moderate score indicates that immediate execution is not advisable and that the transaction should be delayed and reassessed. A high score indicates that automatic public broadcast should be stopped before the transaction becomes visible in the public mempool.
The high-risk outcome is interpreted as a protection against automatic risky submission, not as a permanent removal of user control. When PADGF detects high-risk conditions, the framework does not simply prevent the user from acting. Instead, it presents controlled user options: cancel the transaction, send the transaction through a protected private route, or proceed anyway after receiving a clear warning about potential sandwich-attack exposure. This makes the decision policy more practical because it supports risk-aware intervention while still allowing the user to make the final transaction decision. This decision structure 
turns PADGF from a passive risk-reporting component into an active decision-support framework. The decision engine does not modify Ethereum consensus, reorder transactions, interact with validators, or guarantee future execution safety. Its function is limited to pre-broadcast decision support based on simulation-derived indicators and configured threshold logic.

3.5.2 Controlled Sandwich Attack Simulation
The controlled sandwich attack simulation evaluates the effect of adversarial transaction ordering on the selected victim transaction. This procedure does not monitor a live public mempool or reproduce real-time searcher competition. Instead, it constructs a deterministic transaction-ordering sequence inside the forked environment to isolate the effect of a sandwich-style pattern.
The controlled sequence consists of three ordered stages. First, the attacker performs a front-run transaction before the victim swap, changing the liquidity pool state. Second, the victim transaction is executed under the altered pool condition. Third, the attacker performs a back-run transaction after the victim swap to complete the sandwich ordering structure.
In the official controlled evaluation, the sandwich attack uses the same victim-side parameters as the baseline execution, including the same fork block, DEX protocol, token pair, victim input amount, route, and 1 percent slippage tolerance. This calibration defines the adversarial capital used in the test and does not change the victim-side transaction parameters.
The purpose of this procedure is to measure whether the victim transaction receives a lower output under the adversarial ordering sequence than under the baseline condition. The comparison between the baseline output and the attacked victim output provides the basis for calculating victim output loss and financial loss percentage in Chapter Four. Gas usage for the attacker and victim transactions is also recorded to preserve transaction-level traceability.
This procedure is used as the adversarial reference condition in the evaluation. It does not claim to represent all forms of live MEV behavior, public mempool competition, relay leakage, validator behavior, or builder-level ordering decisions. Its purpose is to create a reproducible adversarial ordering condition under controlled experimental boundaries.
3.5.3 Protected Execution with PADGF
The protected execution procedure evaluates how the PADGF prototype processes the selected transaction before execution. Unlike the baseline path, which records normal execution behavior, and unlike the controlled sandwich attack path, which introduces adversarial ordering, the protected path focuses on the framework’s pre-broadcast simulation, economic risk evaluation, and decision-control workflow. In this procedure, the transaction is first passed to the Transaction Input Module. The framework receives the transaction parameters, including the token pair, input amount, slippage tolerance, and execution route. The transaction is then forwarded to the Pre-Broadcast Transaction Simulation Module, where the framework obtains a reference output and performs local simulation before the transaction is exposed to the public mempool. The simulation outputs are then forwarded to the Risk Evaluation Model. In the revised PADGF prototype, this model evaluates both victim-side transaction impact and attacker-side economic incentive. 
The model calculates victim output loss by comparing the reference output with the simulated output. It then estimates attacker gross profit using the heuristic pool-fee adjustment, calculates builder bribe and attacker retained profit, estimates attacker gas cost 
from the gas condition and attacker gas proxy, and calculates attacker net profit. The model also calculates the profitability ratio and slippage deviation. These values are used to generate the normalized risk score that represents the transaction’s pre-broadcast exposure. The Adaptive Decision Engine then applies the threshold policy to the normalized risk score and returns one of three possible outcomes: Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options. Execution proceeds automatically only when the transaction is classified as low risk. If the transaction is classified as moderate risk, execution is delayed and reassessment is recommended. If the transaction is classified as high risk, automatic public broadcast is stopped and the user is given controlled options: cancel, send privately, or proceed manually with warning.
The protected procedure records the transaction parameters, reference output, simulated output, victim output loss, attacker gross profit, builder bribe, attacker retained profit, attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, profitability ratio, slippage deviation, normalized risk score, PADGF decision, execution permission, and execution status. These values provide the basis for reporting the PADGF protected evaluation results in Chapter Four. This procedure must be interpreted within the prototype boundary. PADGF does not control Ethereum block ordering, modify validator behavior, prevent all MEV strategies, or guarantee final transaction safety. Its role in this evaluation is to demonstrate a client-side decision-support process that evaluates transaction exposure and attacker economic viability before network submission.
3.5.4 Comparative Evaluation Procedure
The comparative evaluation procedure defines how the outputs from the three official paths are interpreted in relation to each other. This subsection does not present the results themselves; it establishes the comparison logic used later in Chapter Four. The comparison begins with the baseline swap execution, which provides the normal reference output. The controlled sandwich attack simulation is then compared with this reference to determine whether the adversarial ordering sequence produced victim-side output degradation. The difference between the baseline expected output and the attacked victim output forms the basis for calculating victim output loss and financial loss percentage. The PADGF protected execution path is interpreted in relation to both previous paths. Its purpose is not to reverse an already executed attack, but to show whether the framework can evaluate transaction conditions before exposure and produce a structured decision. Therefore, the protected path 
is compared using both output-based values and framework-specific values, including simulated output, risk indicators, normalized risk score, and PADGF decision.
The official comparison is restricted to scenarios executed under the same controlled configuration. Maintaining a fixed configuration ensures that observed differences are interpreted as scenario-level effects rather than differences caused by transaction size, token route, slippage setting, or changing network state. 3.6.3 Threshold Policy
The threshold policy defines how the normalized risk score is converted into a PADGF decision. The decision policy uses three risk regions: Execute, Delay, and High-Risk User Options. These regions are defined by two threshold values, denoted as τ1 and τ2. In the implemented prototype, the lower threshold τ1 is set to 0.30 and the upper threshold τ2 is set to 0.70.
The threshold policy is defined as follows:
R < τ1 → Execute
τ1 ≤ R < τ2 → Delay
R ≥ τ2 → High-Risk User Options
where R represents the normalized risk score generated by the Risk Evaluation Model. The Execute region corresponds to low-risk transaction conditions, where attacker net profitability is negligible or absent and transaction-output deterioration is limited. The Delay region corresponds to moderate-risk conditions, where positive attacker incentive or output deterioration suggests that immediate execution may not be advisable and reassessment is required. The High-Risk User Options region corresponds to conditions where the transaction presents substantial economic attractiveness or harmful output degradation. In this region, PADGF stops automatic public broadcast and presents the user with three controlled options: cancel the transaction, send privately through a protected route, or proceed manually after receiving a clear warning.
The weighting and threshold values used in the PADGF prototype are treated as controlled experimental parameters rather than universal Ethereum security constants. The profitability component is assigned a higher weight than the slippage component because sandwich attacks are primarily driven by attacker economic incentive. A transaction with visible output deterioration is not necessarily attractive unless the expected extractable value exceeds the attacker’s execution cost.
The selected 0.6/0.4 weighting therefore reflects the economic priority of attacker net profitability while retaining victim-side output degradation as a substantial component of the score. Similarly, the threshold values τ1 = 0.30 and τ2 = 0.70 are used as prototype decision boundaries to separate low-, moderate-, and high-exposure conditions. These thresholds are not claimed to be globally optimal. They provide a consistent decision policy for controlled evaluation and are validated within the scope of the prototype using representative low-, moderate-, and high-exposure scenarios.
Broader calibration using larger historical datasets and live-network observations remains outside the scope of this prototype and is identified as a direction for future work. 3.6.4 Justification of Risk-Score Weights and Thresholds
The risk-score weighting and threshold values used in PADGF are defined as prototype decision parameters for controlled evaluation rather than universal security constants. The implemented risk score gives greater weight to attacker profitability because sandwich attacks are economically motivated and are only rational when the attacker expects the potential gain to exceed transaction costs and execution uncertainty. For this reason, the profitability component receives a higher contribution than the slippage component in the normalized score. The slippage component remains important because it represents victim-side execution degradation, but slippage alone does not necessarily indicate that a transaction is economically attractive to an attacker.
The selected thresholds provide three operational decision regions for prototype evaluation. The lower threshold separates transactions with limited estimated exposure from transactions that require reassessment, while the upper threshold separates moderate-risk transactions from conditions that justify stopping automatic public broadcast. These boundaries are used to test whether PADGF can consistently translate simulation-derived indicators into structured pre-broadcast decisions. They should therefore be interpreted as configurable prototype parameters rather than fixed Ethereum-wide safety boundaries.
The purpose of using these weights and thresholds is to support repeatable evaluation of the framework under controlled forked-state conditions. In a production deployment, the weighting values and decision thresholds would require calibration using larger datasets, different liquidity pools, transaction sizes, gas regimes, token pairs, and observed market conditions. Therefore, the values used in this study are sufficient for demonstrating the 
feasibility of PADGF as a client-side pre-broadcast decision framework, but they should not be interpreted as final optimized parameters for all Ethereum DeFi environments.

This chapter presented the design, implementation, and evaluation methodology of the PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework (PADGF). The chapter first introduced the phased methodological structure used to guide the development of the framework, connecting framework design, prototype implementation, and controlled evaluation. This structure ensures that PADGF is developed and assessed as a client-side pre-broadcast decision-support framework rather than as a modification to Ethereum consensus rules, decentralized exchange protocols, or deployed smart contracts.
Phase 1 defined the framework design. It established the research scope and client-side objective of PADGF, clarified the system model and threat assumptions, identified the main design principles, and described the architecture of the framework. This phase positioned PADGF as an external pre-broadcast layer that evaluates transaction exposure before the transaction becomes publicly visible in the mempool.
Phase 2 described the prototype implementation. The implemented workflow was organized into four main modules: the Transaction Input Module, the Pre-Broadcast Transaction Simulation Module, the Risk Evaluation Model, and the Adaptive Decision Engine. These modules receive transaction parameters, simulate expected execution under controlled conditions, calculate victim-side and attacker-side evaluation indicators, compute a normalized risk score, and convert that score into an Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options decision.
Phase 3 defined the controlled evaluation procedure. The evaluation was structured around baseline swap execution, controlled sandwich attack simulation, protected execution with PADGF, and comparative evaluation. The baseline path establishes the normal reference condition, the controlled sandwich attack path introduces an adversarial ordering sequence, and the PADGF protected path evaluates the transaction through the framework’s pre-broadcast simulation, economic risk evaluation, and decision-control pipeline.

4.6 Threats to Validity
The results reported in this chapter should be interpreted within the validity boundaries of the implemented PADGF prototype and the controlled forked-state evaluation environment. The evaluation provides evidence that PADGF can process transaction parameters, calculate exposure indicators, and produce structured pre-broadcast decisions under defined experimental conditions. However, as with any controlled prototype-based evaluation, several threats to validity must be considered. These threats do not invalidate the findings, but they define the conditions under which the findings should be understood.
4.6.1 Internal Validity
Internal validity concerns whether the observed results can be attributed to the tested experimental conditions rather than to uncontrolled factors. In this study, internal validity was supported by using a deterministic Ethereum mainnet fork, fixed transaction settings, a defined DEX protocol, a consistent token pair, and recorded transaction outputs. The official result set compared baseline execution, controlled sandwich attack simulation, and PADGF-protected evaluation under a common fork block and transaction context. This structure allowed the study to examine the effect of adversarial ordering and the behavior of the PADGF decision process under controlled conditions.
The updated controlled sandwich attack scenario improves internal validity because it uses the same victim-side parameters as the baseline execution, including the same fork block, DEX protocol, token pair, victim input amount, route, and 1 percent slippage tolerance. Therefore, the comparison between the baseline and controlled sandwich attack is stronger than a comparison based on different victim-side slippage settings. The main experimental 
difference between the baseline and attack scenarios is the introduction of the attacker front-run and back-run sequence.
However, the controlled sandwich attack still depends on a calibrated attacker configuration. The attacker input was set to 60.0 WETH so that the adversarial sequence could complete within the victim’s 1 percent slippage constraint. This means that the result is internally consistent for the tested configuration, but it should not be interpreted as proof that all attacker sizes or all adversarial configurations would produce the same outcome. The attack result shows measurable victim-side degradation under the selected attacker-capital condition, not a universal result for every possible sandwich attack configuration.
Another internal validity consideration is the distinction between victim-side loss and attacker-side profitability. The controlled sandwich attack reduced the victim output from 54,538.403686 USDC to 54,013.908465 USDC, producing a victim output loss of 524.495221 USDC. However, the attacker WETH profit was negative under the tested configuration. This confirms that output degradation and attacker profitability are not identical constructs. The result therefore supports the need to evaluate attacker economics separately from victim loss, but it should not be interpreted as evidence of a profitable attacker strategy under the official attack configuration.
4.6.2 External Validity
External validity concerns the extent to which the findings can be generalized beyond the tested environment. The evaluation was conducted using a deterministic Ethereum mainnet fork, Uniswap V2, the WETH-to-USDC token pair, and selected transaction sizes. This design was appropriate for repeatable testing and controlled comparison, but it does not fully reproduce the conditions of live Ethereum.
In live Ethereum, transaction outcomes may be influenced by rapidly changing liquidity, gas prices, pending public transactions, private order flow, builder behavior, relay selection, competing bundles, and block construction dynamics. The forked evaluation environment cannot fully capture these conditions. Therefore, the results should not be generalized as proof that PADGF will produce the same outputs or decisions across all live Ethereum transactions.
The end-user scenarios increase the breadth of the evaluation by showing how PADGF behaves under low-risk, moderate-risk, and high-risk conditions. However, these scenarios remain supplementary user-facing evidence. They do not replace large-scale testing across 
multiple decentralized exchanges, liquidity pools, token pairs, transaction sizes, market conditions, or live transaction flows. The private-route path is also simulated inside the local Hardhat fork and should not be interpreted as proof of live Flashbots or private-builder inclusion. For this reason, the findings support prototype feasibility and controlled behavioral validation, but not universal deployment readiness.
4.6.3 Construct Validity
Construct validity concerns whether the selected indicators accurately represent the concept being evaluated. In this thesis, sandwich attack exposure was represented using victim-side and attacker-side indicators, including victim output loss, slippage deviation, attacker gross profit, attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, profitability ratio, and normalized risk score. This selection is appropriate because sandwich attack exposure is not determined only by whether the victim receives less output. It also depends on whether the transaction creates an economically viable opportunity for an attacker.
The official controlled sandwich attack result supports this distinction. The victim experienced measurable output degradation, but the attacker profit was negative under the tested gas and capital configuration. This shows why the PADGF model does not rely only on victim output loss or slippage deviation. A transaction may be harmful to the victim while still failing to generate positive attacker profit after costs are considered.
Nevertheless, the normalized risk score should be interpreted as an operational exposure indicator rather than a complete measure of all MEV risk. PADGF does not directly observe live attackers, does not predict final block ordering, and does not capture all private transaction competition. The risk score is produced from simulation-derived values under the configured prototype assumptions. It is therefore suitable for structured pre-broadcast decision support, but it should not be treated as a deterministic probability that a sandwich attack will occur.
The threshold policy also affects construct validity. The Execute, Delay, and High-Risk User Options regions provide a practical decision structure, but the threshold values used in the prototype are experimental parameters. They are useful for evaluating PADGF behavior in a controlled thesis prototype, but they should not be treated as universal Ethereum security constants. Broader empirical calibration would be required before applying the same thresholds across different token pairs, liquidity conditions, transaction sizes, user-risk preferences, or private-routing environments.
4.6.4 Reproducibility Validity
Reproducibility validity concerns whether the evaluation can be repeated under the same conditions and whether similar outputs can be obtained. This study supports reproducibility by reporting the fork block, DEX protocol, token pair, input amounts, slippage settings, gas usage, transaction outputs, risk scores, PADGF decisions, and execution outcomes. The chapter also separates official thesis results from supplementary exploratory results, which helps preserve the clarity of the primary evidence.
However, exact reproducibility depends on preserving the full implementation and execution environment. Changes in the fork block, RPC configuration, contract addresses, token addresses, gas assumptions, dependency versions, execution scripts, or scenario parameters may change the numerical outputs. For example, the official controlled sandwich attack result is tied to the selected fork block, the WETH-to-USDC liquidity state, the 14.0 WETH victim input, the 1 percent victim slippage tolerance, and the 60.0 WETH attacker input. Any change to these parameters may produce a different output loss or attacker-profit result.
The results should therefore be understood as reproducible under the stated configuration, not as fixed values that will necessarily appear under all environments. To strengthen reproducibility, the implementation artifacts, scenario scripts, fork configuration, transaction parameters, and result files should be preserved with the thesis materials. This would allow future researchers to verify the reported results and extend the evaluation under additional conditions.
 
Taken together, these threats to validity define the scope of the Chapter 4 findings. The results provide controlled evidence that PADGF can evaluate transaction exposure, compute relevant indicators, and produce Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options decisions before broadcast. They do not establish universal live-network protection and do not prove live private-relay inclusion. Instead, they support the more defensible conclusion that client-side pre-broadcast evaluation and controlled high-risk handling are technically feasible under prototype conditions and require further testing under broader, 
live, and more adversarial environments.

This chapter presented the results and findings generated by the implemented PADGF prototype. The official thesis results first established a normal execution reference through the baseline scenario, then demonstrated the effect of adversarial transaction ordering through the controlled sandwich attack simulation, and finally reported the PADGF 
protected evaluation under the controlled environment. The baseline execution produced a stable reference output of 54,538.403686 USDC, while the controlled sandwich attack simulation reduced the victim output to 54,013.908465 USDC, producing a measurable output loss of 524.495221 USDC. The attacker profit in the official controlled attack remained negative under the tested configuration, showing that victim-side degradation and attacker-side profitability are analytically distinct.
The end-user results extended the analysis by showing how PADGF responds under low-risk, moderate-risk, and high-risk user-facing conditions. The low-risk case produced Execute Recommended and was executed successfully. The moderate-risk case produced Delay Recommended and was executed after the delay warning was accepted. The high-risk case produced High-Risk User Options, stopped automatic public broadcast, and recorded the selected private-route option. This updated result demonstrates that the high-risk decision is not an absolute user prohibition; it is a protective control that prevents unsafe automatic public submission while preserving user choice.
Overall, the findings indicate that PADGF can operate as a client-side pre-broadcast decision-support framework by evaluating transaction exposure before network submission, computing simulation-derived indicators, and applying a threshold-based decision policy. The results do not establish that PADGF eliminates MEV, guarantees transaction safety, controls live block ordering, or proves live private-relay inclusion. Instead, they demonstrate that the implemented prototype can generate reproducible outputs, measure victim-side degradation under controlled adversarial ordering, estimate attacker economic viability in protected evaluations, and produce structured Execute, Delay, and High-Risk User Options recommendations under the configured policy.

5.1 Introduction
This chapter concludes the thesis by drawing together the main outcomes of the research and clarifying their significance for the PreSend Adaptive Decision Guard Framework (PADGF). The earlier chapters established the problem of MEV and sandwich attacks, reviewed existing mitigation approaches, presented the design and implementation of PADGF, and evaluated its behavior under controlled experimental conditions. The chapter provides a concise overview of the study, discusses the main findings, explains how the research objectives were achieved, and identifies the contributions, implications, limitations, and future research directions. PADGF is not presented as a complete solution to MEV or as a guarantee against all sandwich attacks. Its contribution is more specific: it shows that a prepared transaction can be evaluated before broadcast through a client-side framework that 
estimates exposure indicators and converts them into an Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options decision.
5.2 Research Overview
This research addressed sandwich attack exposure in Ethereum-based decentralized finance transactions. In decentralized exchanges, users submit transactions that interact with automated market maker liquidity pools. Before these transactions are finalized on-chain, they may become
 visible to other network participants. This visibility creates an opportunity for adversarial actors to manipulate transaction ordering by placing transactions before and after the victim transaction. The outcome may be a worse execution result for the victim and an opportunity for the attacker to extract value from the induced price movement.
The study began by examining the broader context of blockchain technology, Ethereum, decentralized finance, MEV, and sandwich attacks. The literature review showed that sandwich attacks are part of a wider transaction-ordering and value-extraction problem rather than isolated execution errors. Existing mitigation approaches provide partial protection, but each approach has limitations. Private relays can reduce exposure to the public mempool, but they introduce infrastructure dependence and trust assumptions. Protocol-level mechanisms may offer stronger structural protection, but they are difficult to deploy and remain outside the control of ordinary users. Smart-contract safeguards and slippage settings can reduce some forms of loss, but they are often static and do not evaluate 
attacker profitability before transaction submission. Post-execution detection can identify harmful behavior after it occurs, but it does not protect the user before execution.
In response to this gap, PADGF was proposed as a client-side pre-broadcast risk evaluation framework. Its purpose is to assess a prepared transaction before it is submitted to the Ethereum network. This placement is central to the study because it moves part of the protection process earlier in the transaction lifecycle. PADGF does not modify Ethereum consensus rules, decentralized exchange protocols, or deployed smart contracts. Instead, it operates as an external decision layer that evaluates whether a transaction should proceed immediately, be delayed for reassessment, or be stopped from automatic public broadcast with controlled high-risk user options.
The prototype was implemented through a modular structure. The Transaction Input Module receives the transaction parameters required for evaluation. The Pre-Broadcast Transaction Simulation Module estimates expected execution behavior under controlled forked-state conditions. The Risk Evaluation Model calculates indicators related to both victim-side loss and attacker-side economic viability, including victim output loss, slippage deviation, attacker gross profit, attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, profitability ratio, and normalized risk score. The Adaptive Decision Engine then applies the configured threshold policy and classifies the transaction into one of three outcomes: Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options.
The evaluation was conducted in a deterministic Ethereum mainnet fork environment. This allowed the prototype to interact with deployed contract logic and existing liquidity conditions while preserving repeatability and avoiding live-network financial risk. The official evaluation followed three main paths: baseline swap execution, controlled sandwich attack simulation, and protected execution with PADGF. End-user scenario outcomes were also used to examine how the decision policy appeared across low, moderate, and high exposure conditions.
The results showed that PADGF can process transaction conditions before broadcast, calculate risk indicators, and produce a structured decision under the configured threshold policy. These results remain bounded by the controlled prototype environment and should not be interpreted as proof of guaranteed protection in all live Ethereum conditions. Rather, the study demonstrates that client-side pre-broadcast evaluation is a viable direction for 
reducing user exposure to sandwich attack conditions through earlier and more informed transaction decision-making.
5.3 Core Findings
The findings indicate that sandwich attack exposure can be assessed before transaction broadcast through a structured client-side decision framework. The results do not show that sandwich attacks can be eliminated at the user side, nor do they suggest that PADGF can control final transaction ordering. They show a more precise outcome: transaction-level exposure can be estimated before submission by combining simulation-derived output behavior with attacker-side economic indicators and threshold-based decision logic.
The baseline execution provided the reference condition for the evaluation. In the official baseline scenario, the swap executed successfully under normal conditions, and the expected output matched the actual output. This established the non-adversarial point of comparison required to identify later degradation. Without such a baseline, the framework would not have a clear basis for deciding whether a simulated output reflected normal execution behavior or increased exposure.
The controlled sandwich attack simulation demonstrated measurable victim-side output degradation. Under adversarial ordering, the victim received less output than in the baseline execution. This confirms the practical relevance of the problem addressed in the thesis: transaction ordering can materially affect user execution outcomes in automated market maker environments. It also supports the decision to examine sandwich attack exposure before broadcast rather than relying only on post-execution detection.
A further finding is that victim loss alone is not sufficient to determine the severity of exposure. The official controlled attack demonstrated output degradation for the victim, but the recorded attacker outcome was not economically profitable under that specific experimental condition. This result is important because lower user output does not automatically mean that the transaction is attractive to a rational attacker. Gas cost, trade size, liquidity conditions, and execution behavior all influence attacker viability. For this reason, PADGF evaluates attacker gross profit, attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, and profitability ratio in addition to victim-side indicators.
The protected evaluation path showed that PADGF can convert transaction exposure into a structured pre-broadcast decision. The framework received transaction parameters, simulated expected execution behavior, calculated risk indicators, and applied the threshold-
based policy to produce a decision. Its value is therefore not limited to identifying unfavorable output. It also translates the evaluation into a practical response: Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options.
The end-user scenario outcomes showed that the decision policy responded consistently as exposure changed. Low-risk conditions led to Execute decisions, moderate-risk conditions led to Delay decisions, and high-risk conditions led to High-Risk User Options, where automatic public broadcast is stopped and the user may cancel, send privately, or proceed manually with a clear warning. These results support the internal logic of the adaptive decision engine and show the practical importance of giving users risk information before transaction submission.
Overall, the findings support the feasibility of PADGF as a client-side pre-broadcast decision-support framework. The evidence remains limited to controlled prototype conditions, but it demonstrates that transaction exposure can be evaluated before broadcast and converted into a structured user-side decision.

5.5 Research Contributions
This thesis contributes to Ethereum transaction risk evaluation by developing and evaluating PADGF as a client-side pre-broadcast framework for assessing sandwich attack exposure. The contribution is not based on changing Ethereum consensus, redesigning decentralized exchange protocols, or claiming to remove MEV from the network. It is based on showing that a transaction can be assessed before submission by combining simulated execution behavior with indicators of attacker economic viability.
The theoretical contribution of the study is the framing of sandwich attack protection as a pre-broadcast decision problem. Much of the existing discussion around MEV mitigation focuses on protocol design, private routing, or post-execution analysis. PADGF shifts attention to the point before submission, where the user or transaction interface can still decide whether to proceed, delay, or block the transaction. This framing strengthens the argument that protection should not only be studied after execution, but also at the stage where transaction exposure begins.
The methodological contribution lies in the evaluation approach used to test the framework. The thesis combined baseline execution, controlled adversarial ordering, PADGF-protected evaluation, and supplementary scenario testing within a deterministic Ethereum mainnet fork. This structure made it possible to compare normal execution, attack-induced degradation, and pre-broadcast decision behavior under repeatable conditions. It also separated official evaluation results from supplementary exploratory scenarios, which improved the clarity of the evidence.
The technical contribution is the modular design and implementation of PADGF. The framework separates transaction input, pre-broadcast simulation, risk evaluation, and adaptive decision-making into distinct components. It also implements a risk model that evaluates both victim-side degradation and attacker-side economic viability. The inclusion of attacker gas cost, attacker net profit, and profitability ratio makes the assessment more informative than a model based only on slippage or output reduction.
The practical contribution is the conversion of risk evaluation into a direct transaction decision. PADGF does not stop at producing numerical indicators. It maps the normalized risk score into Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options outcomes. This makes the 
framework relevant to future wallet-side or client-side transaction interfaces, where users need clear decision support before exposing a transaction to network ordering 
conditions.Taken
 together, these contributions show that client-side pre-broadcast evaluation is a viable direction for reducing user exposure to sandwich attack conditions. PADGF should not be interpreted as a complete MEV-prevention system. Its contribution is narrower and more defensible: it provides a structured method for assessing transaction exposure before broadcast and converting that assessment into a user-side decision.
5.6 Research Implications
The findings of this thesis have implications for academic research and for practical transaction-risk handling in decentralized finance. At the academic level, the study supports the view that MEV mitigation should not be examined only through protocol-level changes, private routing systems, or post-execution detection. The pre-broadcast stage is also an important point of analysis because it is the last stage at which the user or client interface can still make a decision before the transaction is exposed to execution risk.
For research on sandwich attacks, PADGF shows the value of combining victim-side and attacker-side indicators. A transaction may result in lower user output without necessarily creating a profitable opportunity for an attacker. This distinction matters because it prevents the risk model from treating all unfavorable execution outcomes as equally severe. Future MEV research can build on this by giving more attention to attacker viability, gas cost, profitability thresholds, and transaction-level decision support.
The study also has methodological implications. The use of deterministic fork-based evaluation provides a controlled way to compare normal execution, adversarial ordering, and protected evaluation without risking live funds. This approach cannot replace live testing, but it offers a useful stage for framework development before deployment in more uncertain environments. It also shows how prototype-based blockchain security research can produce evidence while maintaining experimental control.
At the practical level, PADGF is relevant to DeFi users, wallet developers, and transaction-interface designers. For users, the framework represents a way to receive risk information before a transaction is submitted. For wallet developers, it suggests that transaction interfaces could move beyond static slippage settings and include simulation-based risk decisions. For researchers and developers working on MEV protection, PADGF provides a 
model for combining transaction simulation, gas estimation, and threshold-based decision logic in a client-side workflow.
These implications should still be interpreted carefully. A practical version of PADGF would not know final block ordering, private order flow, or all competing transactions. Its role would be to improve pre-submission decision-making rather than guarantee execution safety. The value of the framework lies in reducing blind transaction submission, especially for swaps involving large values, unstable prices, or lower-liquidity conditions.

.7 Limitations of the Study
The findings of this thesis must be interpreted within the scope of the implemented prototype and the controlled evaluation environment. PADGF demonstrates the feasibility of pre-broadcast risk evaluation, but it does not provide complete protection against sandwich attacks and does not reproduce all conditions of live Ethereum.
The first limitation concerns the evaluation environment. The prototype was tested using a deterministic Ethereum mainnet fork rather than live mainnet execution. This choice allowed controlled comparison between baseline execution, adversarial ordering, and PADGF-protected evaluation without exposing real funds to risk. It also made the results repeatable. However, a forked environment cannot fully capture the speed and uncertainty of live Ethereum, where liquidity conditions, gas prices, pending transactions, private order flow, and block-building behavior may change within seconds.
The second limitation is the absence of live mempool and private order-flow visibility. PADGF can simulate a prepared transaction and estimate exposure, but it cannot observe all pending public transactions, private bundles, or transactions submitted through protected channels. This limits the framework’s ability to reproduce the full competitive environment in which real MEV strategies operate.
The third limitation concerns final transaction ordering and PBS/builder interaction. In live Ethereum, transaction ordering may be influenced by validators, builders, relays, private submission paths, and competing bundles. PADGF does not interact with this builder market and cannot know the final ordering of a transaction before block inclusion. For this reason, it should be interpreted as a risk-based decision-support framework rather than a deterministic prediction mechanism.
The attacker model is also limited. The study focuses on sandwich attack exposure under controlled adversarial conditions. This is appropriate for the research scope, but it does not 
cover the full range of MEV behavior. Real attackers may use different capital sizes, multi-hop routes, private bundles, changing gas strategies, or more advanced execution patterns. A broader attacker model would be required to test the framework against a wider set of adversarial behaviors.
The threshold policy used in the prototype should also be treated as an experimental design choice. The Execute, Delay, and High-Risk User Options regions provide a practical structure for decision-making, but the threshold values are not universal Ethereum security constants. Different token pairs, liquidity pools, transaction sizes, gas conditions, and user risk preferences may require different calibration. A larger empirical dataset would be needed before the threshold policy could be generalized beyond the prototype setting.
The evaluation scenarios were limited in scale. The official and end-user scenarios were sufficient to test the framework logic and demonstrate different exposure levels, but they do not represent the full diversity of decentralized finance transactions. The framework was not evaluated across a broad set of decentralized exchanges, liquidity pool types, market regimes, token pairs, or long-term live transaction data.
A related limitation concerns the calibrated attacker configuration used in the controlled sandwich attack scenario. The attack was executed using the same victim-side parameters as the baseline scenario, including the same fork block, DEX protocol, token pair, victim input amount, route, and 1 percent slippage tolerance. However, the attacker input was calibrated to 60.0 WETH so that the adversarial sequence could complete under the tested liquidity conditions. This improves comparability between the baseline and attack scenarios, but the result should still be interpreted within the selected fork block, token pair, liquidity state, gas conditions, and attacker-capital configuration.
Another limitation concerns gas estimation. PADGF treats attacker gas cost as part of the attacker’s economic calculation, which is appropriate because gas reduces attacker net profit. However, live gas prices can change quickly, and real attackers may use priority fees, private bundles, or different transaction-submission strategies. As a result, gas estimation in a live deployment would require continuous updating and conservative assumptions.
Finally, PADGF was implemented as a research prototype, not as a production wallet or deployed user-facing application. The thesis evaluates the framework logic and decision model, including a prototype-level private-route handling path for high-risk user options, but it does not include full wallet integration, live private relay inclusion testing, real-time 
monitoring, security auditing, or user-experience validation. These elements would be necessary before the framework could be used as a practical tool by ordinary users.
These limitations do not weaken the central contribution of the thesis; rather, they define its scope. The study shows that client-side pre-broadcast evaluation is technically feasible under controlled prototype conditions. It does not show that PADGF can guarantee protection in every live Ethereum situation.
5.8 Future Research Directions
The findings of this thesis provide several directions for extending PADGF beyond the controlled prototype environment. The most immediate direction is live or near-live testing. The deterministic mainnet fork used in this study was appropriate for repeatability and controlled comparison, but it cannot fully represent the speed and variability of real transaction submission. Further evaluation should therefore incorporate real-time blockchain state, changing gas conditions, continuously updated liquidity data, and repeated simulation close to the moment of broadcast. This would allow PADGF to be tested under conditions that more closely reflect actual Ethereum transaction execution.
A second direction concerns live mempool and private order-flow awareness. PADGF currently evaluates transaction exposure through controlled simulation, but real MEV conditions are shaped by pending public transactions, private bundles, protected submission channels, and rapidly changing liquidity states. A more advanced version of the framework would need to examine how public mempool signals and available private routing information can be incorporated into the risk evaluation process. This would make the assessment closer to the competitive environment in which real transaction ordering takes place.
Builder and PBS-aware evaluation is another important direction. Since post-Merge Ethereum transaction ordering is influenced by validator-builder separation, relays, block builders, and private bundle markets, a live version of PADGF would need to account for these execution-layer realities. Further work could examine how builder behavior, relay selection, bundle inclusion, and private transaction routing affect the reliability of pre-broadcast risk assessment. This direction is particularly important because sandwich attack exposure is not determined only by the transaction itself, but also by the infrastructure through which the transaction is submitted and ordered.
The risk scoring model also requires further refinement. The current PADGF prototype uses a transparent threshold-based policy, which is suitable for a controlled academic prototype because its logic is interpretable and easy to evaluate. Later versions could investigate data-driven or machine learning-based risk scoring if sufficient labeled transaction data becomes available. Any such model would need to remain explainable enough for wallet-side decision-making, since users must be able to understand why a transaction is classified as risky.
Broader evaluation across decentralized exchange designs and token markets is also necessary. The current prototype was evaluated under selected swap conditions, but sandwich attack exposure can vary significantly across token pairs, liquidity depths, transaction sizes, routing paths, and market regimes. Further testing should include different decentralized exchange architectures, including Uniswap V3 concentrated-liquidity pools, low-liquidity pools, volatile market conditions, and multi-hop trading paths. This would help determine whether the risk model generalizes beyond the forked-mainnet scenarios used in this thesis.
The scope of PADGF could also be extended beyond sandwich attacks. Although the framework was designed around sandwich attack exposure, MEV includes other forms of adversarial transaction behavior. Future versions could examine whether the same pre-broadcast evaluation approach can be adapted to front-running, back-running, liquidation-related extraction, or cross-domain arbitrage exposure. Such work would clarify whether PADGF is limited to sandwich attack assessment or whether it can support a broader class of transaction-risk evaluations.
Cross-chain and layer-2 support represents another area for development. Ethereum users increasingly interact with layer-2 networks and other EVM-compatible chains, where sequencing rules, mempool visibility, gas dynamics, and liquidity conditions may differ from Ethereum mainnet. Testing PADGF across these environments would help determine whether the framework can generalize beyond the specific forked-mainnet context used in this thesis.
Finally, further work is needed on wallet-side integration and user-facing design. Since PADGF is intended as a client-side pre-broadcast framework, its practical value depends on whether users can understand and act on its decisions. A production-oriented version would require interface design, warning messages, private submission options, robust error 
handling, stale-simulation protection, security auditing, and user-experience testing. These elements are necessary before the framework can move from a research prototype toward a deployable transaction risk evaluation tool.
Overall, future research should focus on making PADGF more adaptive, more extensively tested, and more closely integrated with real transaction workflows. The present thesis establishes the feasibility of client-side pre-broadcast evaluation. The next stage is to examine whether this approach can remain reliable under the speed, uncertainty, and diversity of live decentralized finance environments.
5.9 Final Conclusion
This thesis investigated whether sandwich attack exposure in Ethereum-based decentralized finance transactions can be assessed before transaction broadcast through a client-side decision framework. To address this question, the research designed, implemented, and evaluated PADGF as a pre-broadcast risk evaluation mechanism that combines simulation-derived transaction behavior with attacker-side economic indicators and threshold-based decision logic.
The results show that PADGF can evaluate prepared transaction conditions, calculate relevant risk indicators, and convert the normalized risk score into Execute, Delay, or High-Risk User Options decisions under controlled prototype conditions. The thesis also shows that evaluating attacker viability is important because victim-side loss alone does not fully explain sandwich attack risk. Gas cost, attacker net profit, and profitability ratio provide necessary context for judging whether a transaction is likely to be economically attractive to a rational adversary.
 
The contribution of PADGF is specific and bounded. It does not eliminate MEV, control block ordering, or guarantee protection against every sandwich attack. Its value lies in moving part of the risk assessment process earlier in the transaction lifecycle, before the user exposes the transaction to network execution conditions. This creates a clearer basis for user-side decision-making and provides a foundation for future wallet-side and real-time transaction risk evaluation systems.
In conclusion, the thesis demonstrates that client-side pre-broadcast evaluation is a feasible and technically grounded direction for reducing sandwich attack exposure. PADGF establishes a structured approach for assessing transaction risk before submission and shows how this assessment can be translated into practical decisions. While further work is required
 
before live deployment, the framework provides a defensible foundation for future research on proactive, simulation-based protection mechanisms in decentralized finance.
